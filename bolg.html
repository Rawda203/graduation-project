Threat Peek

Take a closer look at what’s hiding online.

The Deep Web

includes all content on the internet that is not indexed by traditional search engines like Google or Bing. This means you can't find these pages simply by searching online. Examples of Deep Web content include private databases, academic journals, subscription-based services, personal email accounts, banking portals, online medical records, and internal corporate websites. Accessing the Deep Web usually requires some form of authentication, such as login credentials or a subscription. Unlike the Dark Web, the Deep Web is not inherently dangerous or illegal — it's simply content that is hidden for privacy, security, or practical reasons

The Surface Web

is the portion of the internet that is visible and accessible to the general public — it includes websites like Google, YouTube, Wikipedia, online stores, news sites, and social media platforms. This part of the internet is indexed by search engines, meaning that the content can be easily found through search queries without any special tools or permissions. Although we use the Surface Web every day, it only makes up a small fraction of the entire internet — roughly 4% to 10%. Everything you can access without logging in, subscribing, or using special software is part of the Surface Web. It’s designed for easy access, user-friendliness, and open visibility. The Dark Web

is a concealed segment of the internet that cannot be accessed through regular browsers or search engines. It requires specialized software like Tor (The Onion Router) to enter, which anonymizes user identity and location by routing traffic through a network of encrypted servers. This layer of the internet is often associated with anonymous communication, hidden marketplaces, whistleblower platforms, and forums for political activists in repressive regimes.
